Rooted in San Francisco

Aryan Vats  ·  CS 360  ·  University of San Francisco

SF has over 600 plants on record and each of the plants help contribute to the ecosystem in some way. This project digs into where they come from, what wildlife they support, and when they bloom by focusing on their classification.

Who feeds the pollinators?

Bees, butterflies, hummingbirds — they all need specific plants. Most pollinator plants in SF are native, which makes sense. This city used to be entirely native habitat.

The chart highlights pollinator plants. Hover over any dot to see details.

Where do birds hide?

Dense shrubs and groundcovers give birds and small animals somewhere to shelter. A lot of these are exotics — wildlife will use whatever cover is available.

Now showing cover plants highlighted. Non-cover plants are faded out.

What gets eaten early?

Some plants produce buds and greens that birds and insects eat before most other food is available. These tend to be natives — they've grown alongside local wildlife for a long time.

Showing plants with buds/greens habitat value highlighted.

Who keeps animals fed year-round?

Fruit-producing plants matter most in fall and winter when other food runs out. SF has a decent mix of natives and exotics doing this job.

Fruit-producing plants highlighted. Hover to see bloom season and water needs.

Where do they build?

Nesting plants give birds material and structure to build in. Most of these are natives — the kind SF birds have always nested in.

Nesting plants highlighted. Click the bar chart season labels to filter when you get there.

Spring: peak bloom.

Spring is when most plants are active. SF Natives peak here — they evolved with the local climate and hit their stride early in the year.

Hover any bar to see exact counts. Click a bar to keep it highlighted.

Summer slows down.

SF summers are dry and foggy. Native bloom drops off significantly. Exotics, which often come from Mediterranean or tropical climates, hold up better.

Summer bars highlighted. Notice how exotics stay more active than natives.

Fall: exotics take over.

By fall, most SF Natives have gone quiet. Exotic plants dominate the bloom count. This is when the gap between natives and exotics is biggest.

Fall bars highlighted.

Winter never goes fully quiet.

Even in winter there's still stuff blooming — mostly natives keeping the pollinators fed through the off-season.

Methodology

Data comes from the SF Plant Finder dataset, which lists plants recommended by the city for gardens and green spaces. Each row is a unique species entry with fields for common name, Latin name, plant type, water needs, bloom season, habitat value (ecological role), and climate appropriateness (the origin category used throughout this project).

Duplicates were removed by Latin name, keeping the first occurrence, leaving 629 unique species. The dot matrix, beeswarm, and bar chart all use this deduplicated set. The bar chart counts bloom entries per season including duplicates from the raw file (a plant that blooms in both Spring and Summer gets counted once per season).

The branched tree filters to plants with Water_Needs of None, Low, or Moderate and shows only the top 3 plant types per origin group to keep the layout readable. Plants with missing or irregular water values are excluded from the tree only.

ColorBrewer colors (green sequential + orange) were used for the origin color scale throughout. No AI was used to write the story text. Layout, CSS grid settings, D3 force simulation parameters, and scrollama configuration were done with coding assistance.
